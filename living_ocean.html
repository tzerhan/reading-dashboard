The Hidden Physics of Ocean Life

From the swirling currents around a single embryo to the coordinated dance of a fish school, life in the ocean is shaped by invisible physical forces. This exhibit, a collaboration with Birch Aquarium, explores the surprising connections between marine biology and physics, revealing how principles like flow, coordination, and "handedness" (chirality) create the dynamic patterns of living systems.

The World Around an Embryo

A tiny marine embryo, just a fraction of a millimeter across, is a powerhouse of activity. As it spins, it acts like a microscopic mixer, creating complex flows and currents in the water around it. These flows are crucial for its development, helping it to breathe and sense its environment.

Visualizing the Invisible

Using advanced microscopy and fluorescent particles, scientists can make these invisible currents visible. The image on the left shows a reconstruction of the flow field around a spinning starfish embryo. The lines trace the paths of water molecules, revealing a beautiful and intricate vortex pattern. This is a direct visualization of how a living organism engineers its own physical surroundings.

The Dance of the "Living Crystals"

What happens when thousands of these spinning embryos are placed together? They don't just move randomly. As described in a recent study in Nature, they self-organize into stunning, large-scale patterns, forming a "living chiral crystal."

From Single Spins to Collective Swirls

  • Chiral Motion: Each starfish embryo spins in a specific direction (clockwise or counter-clockwise), a property called chirality or "handedness".
  • Hydrodynamic Coupling: The flow field created by one embryo influences its neighbors. This "communication" through the water coordinates their motion.
  • Emergent Order: Over time, these local interactions lead to global order. The embryos arrange themselves into a crystal-like lattice, with neighboring embryos spinning in opposite directions, creating vast arrays of tiny, swirling vortices.

Simulating a Swarm

How do simple rules lead to complex collective behavior like fish schooling or bird flocking? Physicists use simple computer models to explore this question. The Vicsek model is one of the most famous examples. In this model, each "particle" tries to align with its neighbors, but with a little bit of randomness or "noise".
